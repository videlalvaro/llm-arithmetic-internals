# ACM Queue Pitch Abstract

How would you do arithmetic if you had no fingers, no abacus, no scratch paper, and no written columns, only matrices?

Large language models can answer arithmetic questions, but the interesting question is not whether they can be wrapped with a calculator. They can. The interesting question is what kind of number sense appears inside a system whose body is made of vectors, attention maps, residual streams, and learned projections. Human arithmetic grew out of bodies, objects, marks, and spatial metaphors. A transformer gets a different body, and therefore a different arithmetic.

This article tells that story through a concrete debugging trail. We start with the familiar temptation to "just call Python," then tighten the question until the prompt text is no longer available. If a calculator is called, its arguments must come from the model's internal activations. That boundary turns a product trick into a scientific test: did the model's hidden state carry the operation and operands, or did the experiment smuggle them in?

The arithmetic that comes out of that matrix body looks less like written columns and more like geometry. Numbers appear as directions and rotations, a little like positions on a clock or points on a spiral. Longer answers have to be broken into chunks and printed from left to right, which gives the model failure modes unlike the ones we learn on paper. Rune uses those ideas to confirm and explain a line of recent research: models can carry number-like structure inside their activations. In one frozen Llama model, that structure was readable enough to recover operations and operands for several arithmetic tasks without parsing the prompt text. The result is not a new theory from scratch; it is a careful, practitioner-facing account of what this kind of internal arithmetic looks like, where it works, and where its resolution runs out.

The piece is written for practitioners who want a clear map of modern interpretability tools without a specialist glossary. Arithmetic provides the thread: residual streams as scratchpads, probes as measuring instruments, sparse autoencoders as feature dictionaries, activation patching as causal evidence, steering as intervention, and provenance audits as the line between discovery and self-deception. The larger lesson is about doing careful science with systems that are easy to fool yourself about: remove the prompt text, remove the labels, add the awkward negatives, and keep narrowing the claim until what remains is small enough to be true.
